Set Theory

  • The studies of things that have stuff in them. It sounds silly, and that's because it somewhat is.
  • Set theory is the basis of all mathematics, and a field of study in its own right
  • It posits a few axioms (statements that we define as true), and examines what is possible from those axioms
  • Due to its fundamental nature, set theory is very closely related to pure logic, philospohy, and theoretical computer science

Real Analysis

  • Real analysis is essentially the formalization of calculus
  • The most important aspect of this formalization is that of the limit
  • Most first semester Real Analysis courses formalize all you would learn in Calculus I and II
  • Later semesters generalize the results to multiple dimensions, infinite dimensions, and even on things that aren't real numbers at all

Abstract Algebra

  • The generalization of properties of operations, such as a+b=b+a
  • Abstract Algebra asks "If I have a set with an operation that does this, how much do I know about the set and operation?"
  • This question is condensed into studying the properties of algebraic structures such as groups, rings, fields, and modules
  • Algebra has many applications you wouldn't think of at first glance, such as in physics, solving polynomials, and Rubik's cubes!

Measure

  • Can be considered a more advanced version of Real Analysis
  • A measure is a generalization of the notion of length/volume to more wacky shapes
  • Measure theory was created to do things that the usual Real Analysis catalog is incapable of, such as integrating certain functions
  • Measure theory is also central to probability theory: a probability space is an example of a measure space
